Executive Summary

Green energy refers to electricity generated from renewable and environmentally sustainable sources such as solar, wind, hydropower, geothermal, and biomass. The global shift toward renewable energy is driven by the need to reduce greenhouse-gas emissions, improve energy security, and support sustainable economic development. This report provides a concise technical overview of global green energy trends, technologies, and challenges.

Renewable energy capacity has grown rapidly over the past decade. Solar and wind are currently the fastest-expanding sources due to declining costs and technological improvements. Hydropower remains the largest renewable electricity source globally, while solar photovoltaic (PV) installations account for most new capacity additions. Many countries have committed to net-zero emissions targets by 2050, accelerating investments in clean energy infrastructure and grid modernization.

Key green energy sources

  • Solar Energy: Converts sunlight into electricity using photovoltaic panels; widely adopted due to scalability and decreasing costs.
  • Wind Energy: Uses turbines to generate electricity; major growth in onshore and offshore wind farms
  • Hydropower: Produces electricity from flowing water; provides stable baseload power but depends on geography and water availability.
  • Geothermal Energy: Utilizes underground heat; reliable but limited to suitable regions.

Green energy development creates jobs in manufacturing, installation, and maintenance while reducing dependence on fossil fuels. Renewable power generation significantly lowers carbon dioxide emissions and air pollution. The levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) for solar and wind has declined, making renewables competitive with or cheaper than fossil-fuel-based generation in many regions.

Challenges experienced in that sector

  1. High upfront infrastructure and storage costs
  2. Intermittent power supply from solar and wind
  3. Limited grid capacity in developing regions
  4. Policy and regulatory inconsistencies
  5. Need for improved energy storage technologies

Global renewable energy capacity is expected to continue expanding through 2030 and beyond. Advances in battery storage, green hydrogen, and smart grid systems will enhance reliability and efficiency. International cooperation, supportive policies, and sustained investment are essential to meeting climate targets and ensuring universal access to clean energy.
